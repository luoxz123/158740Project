# Individual Reflective Essay

My role in the NZ Renewable Energy Suitability Explorer project was mainly as the project planner, technical integrator, spatial database and deployment lead, and final troubleshooter. I was not the main person responsible for the frontend design or for the GIR text extraction work. Instead, my contribution focused on making the whole project work as one operational WebGIS product: creating the master plan, helping the group divide work, advising other members when their parts needed to connect to the wider system, integrating the outputs, tuning the database and spatial analysis, preparing deployment on the VM, writing the final report, and helping record the video presentation.

At the start of the project, I felt the most important task was to create a clear master plan. The assignment required more than a simple map; it needed a working WebGIS with spatial data, PostGIS, GeoServer, analysis, search, popups, layer control, and evidence of design decisions. Because of this, I first broke the project into major components: frontend mapping, data collection, spatial analysis, GIR, database and GeoServer, deployment, documentation, and presentation. This helped the group understand that the project was not only about making a map look good, but about building a connected system. After setting out this plan, I let members choose tasks that matched their interests and confidence. Jimmy focused on the frontend design and database access from the web map, Ishraka focused on the GIR component, Pujia focused on downloading existing LINZ and spatial datasets, and I took responsibility for the overall plan, integration, database work, spatial modelling, deployment, final tuning, report writing, and problem solving.

The process was partly successful. The successful part was that the group did eventually produce a functional WebGIS application rather than just separate pieces of work. We had a working Leaflet interface, PostGIS database, GeoServer connection, renewable suitability layers, transmission lines, protected areas, weather resource data, GIR locations, and ranked candidate sites. I also think the master plan was useful because it gave the project a structure and made it easier to identify missing requirements near the end. Without that structure, we might have built only a frontend map without enough analysis or database evidence.

However, the process was not perfect. The main weakness was that integration happened later than it should have. Different members worked on their sections separately, and some outputs were not immediately in the correct format or coordinate reference system for the final system. This meant I had to spend a lot of time near the end doing "firefighting": fixing database schemas, checking GeoJSON files, adjusting SQL scripts, improving deployment batch files, adding missing processed data, and making sure the frontend, database, and GeoServer layers all pointed to the right locations. If I were doing the project again, I would create integration milestones much earlier. For example, by the end of week one every member should provide one small working sample in the exact final format. By the middle of the project, all layers should already load into PostGIS and the frontend, even if the analysis is still incomplete. This would reduce last-minute pressure and make quality control easier.

For the work I personally carried out, I am most satisfied with the integration and final operational structure. The project became much stronger after the database schema, deployment scripts, processed data folders, and documentation were made consistent. I also developed the site-selection approach using weather resource data, distance to transmission lines, GIR evidence, and interpolation confidence. Because high-resolution commercial solar and wind datasets were not affordable for a student project, I used the available 87-location weather dataset and applied interpolation to estimate wider spatial patterns. This was not perfect, but it was a realistic and transparent solution under the project constraints. I also added a stronger transmission-line dataset from Transpower open data so that candidate sites were not only based on weather but also on connection feasibility.

The weakest part of my own work is probably the simplified suitability model. The final scoring method is useful for screening, but it is not an engineering-grade renewable energy model. It does not include detailed land ownership, slope, grid capacity, consenting constraints, turbine wake modelling, solar panel orientation, or long-term measured radiation from dense weather stations. The interpolation is only as good as the available input locations. Some remote areas may still be over- or under-estimated because weather conditions can change sharply across mountains and coastlines. I understand this limitation, and I think it is better to explain it honestly than to pretend the results are definitive. The output should be treated as a planning-level decision support tool, not as a final answer for where to build wind farms or solar farms.

I was particularly proud of turning a messy collection of files and ideas into a deployable project. At several points, the project felt unstable because scripts worked on one machine but not another, command line tools behaved differently on Windows, and the VM had limitations such as memory and missing software. I helped solve these issues by preparing Windows batch files, cleaning the repository for GitHub upload, improving database import scripts, and writing instructions for restarting the frontend and updating the VM. I also spent time checking why some datasets appeared in psql but not in pgAdmin, why GeoServer ports mattered, and why transmission line geometries could fail database constraints. These practical fixes were not always visible in the map, but they were essential for making the product usable.

For the future, I would recommend three main improvements. First, the weather resource dataset should be expanded with more authoritative station or gridded data if funding or access is available. NIWA VCSN or other high-resolution datasets would improve the solar radiation and wind speed evidence, although cost was a real limitation in this project. Second, the suitability model should include more constraints, especially slope, land ownership, grid capacity, distance from settlements, and planning restrictions. Third, the system should have a stronger backend API instead of relying mainly on static GeoJSON outputs and WMS layers. A backend API would allow dynamic filtering, live database queries, and better performance for larger datasets.

The most useful part of doing this project was learning how many small technical decisions are needed to make a WebGIS actually work. In theory, it is easy to say that the system will use Leaflet, PostGIS, GeoServer, and Python. In practice, every part needs careful connection: coordinate systems must match, geometry must be valid, database schemas must be clear, file paths must work on the VM, and the web map must load data from the correct source. I learned that integration is not a final small task; it is one of the central parts of the project.

The most important lesson I learned was that a working system is more valuable than an overcomplicated design that cannot be deployed. At first, it was tempting to include many advanced datasets and functions. However, some data sources were expensive, some websites were difficult to scrape reliably, and the VM could not support a heavy Docker deployment. I had to make practical decisions: use open-source software, use available data, simplify the model where necessary, and make the deployment repeatable. This taught me that good project management requires technical ambition but also honest limits.

From my perspective, the group allocation was clear but the workload was not completely even. Jimmy carried the frontend design and map interaction work, including the visual layout and connection of data outputs to the user interface. This was an important part because the final product needed to be understandable and professional for users. Ishraka worked on the GIR section, especially the concept of extracting renewable-energy-related locations from web text. This gave the project a second method beyond ordinary spatial datasets. Pujia helped with existing spatial data collection, especially finding and downloading LINZ-related datasets and preparing some base GIS resources. My own work covered the project plan, integration, database structure, spatial analysis, weather resource processing, transmission line improvement, deployment support, final tuning, report writing, and video presentation preparation.

I think my role was therefore both technical and organisational. Technically, I connected the pieces into one system and filled gaps when some required components were missing or not ready. Organisationally, I tried to keep the assignment requirements visible and remind the group what still needed to be done. I also gave advice to other members when their work needed to match the database, frontend, or final report. This role was sometimes stressful because integration problems often appeared close to the deadline, but it also helped me understand the whole product more deeply than if I had only worked on one isolated component.

Overall, I believe the project was successful as a student WebGIS project because it produced an operational renewable energy suitability explorer with real spatial data, analysis, database support, GeoServer integration, and a clear explanation of limitations. It is not perfect, and the suitability results should be improved with better resource data and more planning constraints in future work. However, I am proud that the final product is not just a mock-up. It can be deployed, explored, and explained, and it demonstrates the main skills required in the assignment: WebGIS design, spatial data processing, database integration, and reflective understanding of the design process.
